Abstract

Foreign ownership of U.S. securities has grown from a bilateral Japan-and-China story into one of the more structurally interesting questions in international finance. Using the TIC SHL survey across 247 economies from 2002 to 2024, five findings emerge: (1) the 2008 Global Financial Crisis permanently shifted the level of foreign Treasury demand, reducing the annual growth rate from 16.3% to 6.0% — a break confirmed by the Chow test (F = 17.59, p < 0.001); (2) foreign creditor concentration fell by 47%, significantly reducing rollover risk; (3) China has divested approximately $36 billion in U.S. Treasuries per year since its 2011 peak, with the UK, Canada, France, and Korea statistically shown to be absorbing those outflows; and (4) the foreign portfolio has rotated sharply from bonds toward U.S. equity, with the equity share rising from 32% to 55% over the study period.

01

Did 2008 Change Everything?

Log-Linear OLS Regression  ·  Chow Structural Break Test

Foreign Treasury holdings grew from $0.91 trillion in 2002 to $7.11 trillion by 2024 — 683% over 22 years. The question is whether that trajectory is one story or two. A log-linear model lets us read the slope as an approximate annual growth rate. The Chow test then asks whether the pre- and post-2008 slopes are statistically distinguishable — a large F-statistic with a small p-value means the two regimes are genuinely different, not just noisy variation around a single trend.

Key Finding

The Chow test rejects parameter stability with high confidence (F = 17.59, p = 0.000048). Before the crisis, foreign Treasury holdings grew at 16.3% per year — a pace driven by dollar reserve accumulation in China, Japan, and commodity-exporting economies. That rate fell to 6.0% per year after 2008. The level shifted upward permanently, consistent with the safe-asset shortage literature, but the growth rate did not recover. The GFC raised the floor of foreign demand while the simultaneous expansion of Treasury supply capped the subsequent pace.

02

Is the U.S. Too Dependent on a Few Lenders?

Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI)  ·  Linear Trend Regression

A concentrated creditor base creates rollover risk. If one or two countries hold the majority of foreign-owned debt and either sells aggressively, the market impact can be significant. The HHI — the standard concentration measure in industrial organisation, and the same tool the DOJ uses in merger reviews — quantifies this directly. Each country's share of total foreign Treasury holdings is squared and summed annually; higher scores indicate more concentration.

Key Finding

Concentration has fallen by nearly half in 22 years — a statistically significant and consistent decline (p < 0.000001, R² = 0.700). Japan's share fell from 29% to 14%; China's peaked at 33% in 2010 before declining to 11% by 2024. The gap has been filled by the UK, India, Canada, France, and Korea — a more dispersed set of creditors with different policy motivations, time horizons, and domestic constraints. From a debt management standpoint this diversification is structurally positive, though it also means Treasury must manage a broader and less predictable set of relationships.

03

China Is Selling — But Who Is Buying?

OLS Trend Regression  ·  Pearson Correlation  ·  Linear Projection

China's reduction from a $1.302 trillion peak in 2011 to $0.753 trillion by 2024 is the most-discussed development in foreign Treasury markets. The practical question — whether this creates a demand gap — turns on whether other buyers are systematically stepping in. A sustained negative Pearson correlation between China's portfolio share and another country's share is direct evidence of substitution.

Key Finding

The divestment is real and statistically robust — $36 billion per year with a tight confidence interval and R² = 0.786. At that pace, China's holdings reach roughly $660 billion by 2030. The more important result is on the substitution side. The UK (r = −0.702), France (r = −0.657), Canada (r = −0.589), and Korea (r = −0.549) all show strong negative correlations with China's share — systematic, not coincidental. Japan and Taiwan show no correlation; their holdings are driven by exchange rate and monetary policy considerations, not by absorbing Chinese outflows. The market is handling China's exit without a structural demand shortfall.

04

From Bonds to Stocks — The Portfolio Rotation

Linear Trend Regression  ·  Welch Two-Sample t-Test

The TIC survey captures all U.S. long-term securities, not just Treasuries. U.S. equity markets significantly outperformed fixed income over the past two decades, and the global expansion of passive index investing mechanically increased the weight of U.S. stocks in foreign portfolios. Tracking each asset class as a share of total foreign holdings and testing whether the shift is statistically real requires comparing two eras with different variances — which is why the Welch t-test is used here rather than its standard version.

Key Finding

The equity share rose 22 percentage points at a statistically significant rate of +1.05 pp/year (p < 0.000001, R² = 0.735). The Welch t-test confirms the early and late periods are genuinely distinct (t = −6.61, p < 0.00001). The Treasury share has no significant trend (p = 0.170) — Treasuries grew in dollar terms but were outpaced by U.S. equity appreciation. The U.S. is now financed more through foreign equity ownership than at any point in the TIC survey's history — a qualitatively different external liability that carries no rollover risk.

05

Are There Natural Groups of Countries?

Principal Component Analysis (PCA)  ·  K-Means Clustering

With 247 countries and 23 time periods, the data is high-dimensional enough that patterns are not visible without reduction. PCA compresses the country-by-time share matrix into a small number of components that capture the dominant axes of variation. K-Means clustering then operates on those components to group countries by behavioral similarity — no labels assigned in advance, no assumptions about how many groups there should be beyond what the elbow method suggests.

Key Finding

PC1 captures 93% of the total variance — essentially a size ranking — while PC2 captures trajectory shape. Japan and China sit on opposite sides of the PC2 axis: Japan's long, slow decline from a dominant position versus China's sharp rise and subsequent reversal. K-Means isolates both as singleton clusters, confirming statistically what is evident qualitatively — no other country behaves like either of them. The 15-country Cluster 0 represents the multi-polar structure filling the space left by China's retreat. Treating all clusters as one group in a monitoring framework would obscure more than it reveals.

Taken together, these findings describe a U.S. external financing landscape that is more stable in some respects — the creditor base is wider and concentration risk is lower — but more complex in others. China's divestment is being absorbed, but it is a dynamic that requires continued monitoring. The equity rotation is structural and likely to persist. Each of these shifts has direct implications for debt management, reserve adequacy assessment, and geopolitical risk analysis.
